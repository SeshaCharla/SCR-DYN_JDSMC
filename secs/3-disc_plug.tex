A key observation that guided the modeling approach was that the residence time of the plug-flow reaction in the SCR-ASC
chamber is considerably shorter than the sampling time of the sensors used to measure gas concentrations in the truck.
This fact is verified by computing the mean residence time of the reactants using the dimensions of the chamber and the
flow-rate under constant temperature conditions in the truck and test-cell data. Consequently, the sensors capture only
the averaged effects of the underlying reacting flow dynamics due to their lower sampling frequency. This phenomenon was
explicitly considered in the development of the discrete nonlinear model for the SCR-ASC system.

Considering the reaction chamber as a control volume, the dynamic model is developed based on the molar conservation of
species at the inlet and outlet separated by a residence-time interval. By assuming a zero-order hold on the inlet
species over the sampling time, the equations relating inlet and outlet concentrations at the residence-time scale are
extended to the sampling-time scale. This procedure is repeated for the catalyst saturated with adsorbed ammonia, where
the reaction dynamics are independent of urea dosing, resulting in a switched system.

By introducing a new quantity, $\eta$, defined as the change in $NO_x$ concentration due to reduction, we can eliminate
unobservable states (surface concentration of adsorbed ammonia) and formulate the model for $NO_x$ reduction dynamics as
a function of measured inputs and states.
